The Looming Theft: Artisan Weaves in Paris

Tracing the distinct geometric patterns of Andean textiles that were paraded on the Fall '23 runways without a single citation to the indigenous weavers.

By Dr. Elara Vance
October 10, 2024 • 12 min read

When the first model stepped onto the runway at the Grand Palais during the highly anticipated Fall '23 collection, a familiar gasp echoed not just through the Parisian audience, but across the globe among textile scholars and indigenous communities.

The collection, lauded by mainstream critics for its "innovative geometric layering" and "raw, earthy modernism," was in fact a direct, uncredited replication of traditional Andean weaving techniques. The patterns—intricate, diamond-shaped motifs known as pallay—are not merely aesthetic choices; they are complex woven languages documenting agricultural cycles, regional identities, and spiritual cosmologies.

The Geography of Inspiration

Fashion houses have long utilized the ambiguous term "inspiration" as a shield against accusations of appropriation. However, there is a distinct line between respectful homage and extractive capitalism. In this collection, the specific tension of the warp, the natural dye colors, and the symbolic geometry were lifted directly from communities in the Sacred Valley of Peru.

"To take our patterns without our name is to take our voice. These are not just designs; they are our ancestors' books."

What makes this specific instance so egregious is the complete erasure of the originators. The press release distributed to attendees cited "abstract architectural forms" and "the rugged terrain of the designer's imagination." Nowhere were the Quechua weavers mentioned. Nowhere were the centuries of refinement credited.

Reclaiming the Narrative

This is not a new story in haute couture. From Navajo prints slapped onto fast-fashion underwear to Maasai beadwork rebranded as generic "safari chic" by luxury conglomerates, the Global North has consistently treated the cultural heritage of the Global South as an open-source mood board.

We must demand better transparency in the supply chain of ideas. True luxury does not steal; true luxury collaborates, credits, and compensates. Until fashion houses are held accountable for the provenance of their designs, the runway remains a site of continuous colonial extraction.
